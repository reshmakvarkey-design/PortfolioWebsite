Revolutionizing Pharma Submissions with AI

Role Product Designer
Type SaaS, AI/ML
Focus UI/UX, Interaction Design

Overview

From 25 weeks manual drafting to an 8 week, AI assisted submission draft workflow in a global pharma organization.

Problem Statement

Regulatory submission authoring is typically slow because writers must reconcile scattered data sources, enforce CTD/eCTD structure, and maintain cross‑document consistency while meeting aggressive filing targets. In many organizations, the initial draft alone consumes months, even though the overall industry is now aiming for single‑digit week submission timelines.

Pharmaceutical companies spend months preparing regulatory submission documents, with 80% of the time consumed by manual formatting, data compilation, and compliance checking.

Our Process

Primary Users

User Research: Interviewing Users

To understand the real bottlenecks in our 25–16 week submission process, I conducted six in-depth interviews with medical writers, regulatory leads, and quality reviewers. Each 60-minute session combined open-ended questions with walkthroughs of their latest submissions, so I could see both their tools and their workarounds in context.

I focused on four areas: how they gathered source documents, how they used templates today, how review cycles actually unfolded, and what scared them about AI-generated content. This helped me separate ‘noise’ (complaints about generic tooling) from critical needs tied to compliance and traceability.

Customer Journey Mapping

This customer journey map illustrates how regulatory writers collaborate with the platform from the moment they see existing submissions to the final download of a regulator‑ready document. It emphasizes not just the linear steps (configure template, map sources, generate, edit, export) but also the agentic layer: where the AI proactively recommends templates, suggests source documents, surfaces insights, maintains confidence scores, preserves manual edits during regeneration, and learns from user feedback over time.

By framing the journey this way, your case study shows how the product transforms a manual, error‑prone process into a supervised, AI‑assisted workflow that still keeps writers in full control of compliance and narrative quality.

User Needs (Impact Feasibility Matrix)

The Impact-Feasibility Matrix is a strategic tool used to prioritize features, initiatives, or design decisions by evaluating their potential impact against the effort or complexity required for implementation. In this project, the matrix helped balance high-value AI-driven capabilities, such as inline reference traceability and agentic chat iteration, with regulatory constraints and technical feasibility.

This approach ensured that development focused first on features that would deliver maximum benefit to regulatory writers in terms of time savings, trust, and compliance, while managing risks and resource constraints effectively. Presenting this matrix in the case study demonstrates your analytical thinking and product prioritization skills in a complex, regulated environment.

Usability Testing

The goal of usability testing was to validate whether regulatory writers and leads could comfortably work with the AI co‑pilot across the full drafting workflow—from template setup to AI‑assisted iterations—without sacrificing control or trust. The focus was on learnability, transparency of AI behavior, and efficiency in completing realistic authoring tasks.

Participants and Setup

  • 6 participants across roles: Senior medical writers (primary users), Regulatory affairs leads, Quality / CMC reviewers
  • Experience range: 5–15 years in regulatory submissions
  • Testing format: 60–90 minute moderated sessions conducted remotely with screen‑sharing
  • Materials: High‑fidelity prototype with realistic submission templates, mapped sources, and sample AI outputs; Scenario scripts based on an upcoming submission.

What's Next and Key Learning

What's Next

  • Extend agent capabilities beyond drafting into pre‑submission readiness checks, automatically scanning for typical regulator queries, missing justifications, or region‑specific requirements before handoff.
  • Deepen integrations with upstream and downstream systems (clinical data repositories, document management, publishing tools) so the workspace becomes a single source of truth across the full submission lifecycle.
  • Introduce role‑based views and dashboards for regulatory leads and quality reviewers, summarizing risk areas, insight resolution status, and submission readiness at a glance.
  • Add in‑product onboarding and “safe AI” education tailored to regulatory users, helping new teams understand what the agent can and cannot do, and how to supervise it effectively.
  • Extend the drafting capabilities to other document types.

Key Learnings

  • Control & Traceability: Designing for regulated, expert users means optimizing for control, traceability, and explainability first; speed is only valuable when users can see and trust how the system reached its output.
  • Collaborator, Not Oracle: Generative AI is most successful when framed as a collaborator, not an oracle: the biggest shift was moving writers from “authoring from scratch” to “curating and supervising a smart first draft.”
  • Details Matter: Small interaction details—like visible mapping status, explicit chat context, and clear version history—had an outsized impact on trust and adoption compared to more “flashy” AI features.
  • Scenario-Based Testing: Early, scenario‑based usability testing with real regulatory users was critical: it surfaced non‑obvious risks (like fear of hidden automation) and led directly to guardrails, confirmations, and UI language that made the agent feel safe to use in real submissions.
